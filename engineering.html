Advanced Prompt Engineering

Go beyond basic prompts. Learn structured techniques to build reliable, accurate, and complex AI-powered systems.

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting

CoT prompting guides the AI to break down a multi-step problem into a series of intermediate reasoning steps. Instead of jumping to a conclusion, the model "thinks out loud," which significantly improves its performance on complex arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning tasks.

When to Use:

  • Math word problems.
  • Multi-step logic puzzles.
  • Any task where the final answer depends on a sequence of correct intermediate steps.
Q: A farmer has 15 cows and 25 chickens. How many legs are there in total on the farm?

A: Let's break this down step by step.
1. First, calculate the number of cow legs. Cows have 4 legs each. So, 15 cows * 4 legs/cow = 60 legs.
2. Next, calculate the number of chicken legs. Chickens have 2 legs each. So, 25 chickens * 2 legs/chicken = 50 legs.
3. Finally, add the total legs together. 60 cow legs + 50 chicken legs = 110 legs.
The answer is 110.

Expected AI Behavior

By providing this detailed example, you are teaching the model a pattern. When you give it a new, similar problem, it will follow the same step-by-step reasoning process to generate an accurate answer.

Self-Consistency

This technique builds on Chain-of-Thought. Instead of generating just one reasoning path, you prompt the model multiple times with the same question (using a higher temperature for variability). You then select the most common answer from the different reasoning paths. This democratic approach helps to marginalize reasoning errors and improves accuracy on complex tasks.

When to Use:

  • High-stakes reasoning tasks where accuracy is paramount.
  • Arithmetic and commonsense reasoning problems.
# Step 1: Prompt the AI with a CoT question multiple times.

Prompt: "Q: There are 15 trees in the grove. Grove workers will plant trees daily. After 1 day, they have 20 trees. After 2 days, they have 25 trees. How many trees will they have after 5 days?"

# Step 2: Analyze the multiple outputs.

Output 1: ...They add 5 trees per day. After 5 days, they will have 15 + 5*5 = 40 trees.
Output 2: ...The pattern is +5 trees each day. Day 3: 30, Day 4: 35, Day 5: 40. The answer is 40.
Output 3: ...The increase is 5 trees. 15 + (5*5) = 40. The answer is 40.

# Step 3: Select the most common answer.
The most frequent answer is 40.

Expected AI Behavior

The model generates several different, plausible reasoning paths. By taking the majority vote, you can be more confident in the final answer, even if one of the paths contained a minor error.

Tree of Thoughts (ToT)

Tree of Thoughts extends Chain-of-Thought by allowing the model to explore multiple reasoning paths at once, like branches on a tree. At each step, the model can generate several different "thoughts" or next steps, and then evaluate them to decide which path is most promising to continue exploring. This is much more powerful than a single CoT path for problems that require exploration or strategic planning.

When to Use:

  • Creative writing tasks where multiple plot points could be explored.
  • Strategic games like chess or Go.
  • Problems with no single, obvious path to a solution.
# This is a conceptual example, as a true ToT system requires more complex code.

Initial Prompt: "I have a character, a detective, at a crime scene. The victim was poisoned. What are three different ways the detective could start the investigation?"

Step 1: Generate Thoughts
Thought 1: Interview the family immediately.
Thought 2: Analyze the poison to identify its origin.
Thought 3: Search the victim's office for enemies or clues.

Step 2: Evaluate Thoughts
Prompt: "Evaluate the pros and cons of these three starting points for a mystery novel."

Step 3: Select and Expand
Based on the evaluation, the writer (or the AI) chooses the most promising path (e.g., Thought 2) and generates further thoughts from there.

Expected AI Behavior

The model doesn't just follow one line of reasoning. It actively explores and prunes a "tree" of possibilities, leading to more robust and creative problem-solving. This is a more deliberate and comprehensive approach than a single-path CoT.

Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)

RAG enhances LLMs by grounding them in external knowledge. Before answering a prompt, the system retrieves relevant information from a specified knowledge base (like a company's internal documents, a textbook, or a set of recent articles). This retrieved context is then provided to the LLM along with the original question, allowing it to generate answers that are more accurate, up-to-date, and verifiable.

When to Use:

  • Building question-answering systems over private documents.
  • Reducing factual hallucinations and providing citations.
  • Answering questions about recent events not in the model's training data.
# This is a conceptual example of the final prompt sent to the LLM.

Original Question: "What were the key features announced for the new 'Spectra' phone model?"

--- Retrieved Context from internal documents ---
Document 1: The 'Spectra' features a new 'Photonic Chip' for 50% faster image processing.
Document 2: The battery life of the 'Spectra' has been extended to 48 hours of typical use.
Document 3: The 'Spectra' will be available in three new colors: 'Cosmic Blue', 'Starlight Silver', and 'Meteorite Gray'.
---

Final Prompt to LLM:
"Based on the following context, what were the key features announced for the new 'Spectra' phone model?

Context: [Paste Retrieved Context here]

Answer:"

Expected AI Output

The key features announced for the new 'Spectra' phone model include the 'Photonic Chip' for faster image processing, an extended battery life of 48 hours, and three new colors: 'Cosmic Blue', 'Starlight Silver', and 'Meteorite Gray'.

Structuring Prompts with Delimiters

Clearly separating different parts of your prompt is crucial for avoiding ambiguity. Delimiters, such as triple backticks (```), XML tags (<tag></tag>), or even simple markers like `###`, help the model distinguish between instructions, context, examples, and user input. This simple trick can significantly improve the reliability of your prompts.

When to Use:

  • In almost all complex prompts.
  • When providing text for the model to summarize, analyze, or transform.
  • When building prompts programmatically that include user input, to prevent prompt injection.
